Page 1: For his community service project, Marcus chose to volunteer at the local animal shelter every Saturday morning for a month. He had never been to a shelter before.
Page 2: On his first visit, the shelter worker showed him around. There were dogs, cats, and even a few guinea pigs. Every animal had a card on its kennel with its name and story.
Page 3: Marcus was given the job of cleaning water bowls and refilling them with fresh water. It was not exciting, but the shelter worker said it was one of the most important jobs.
Page 4: He also helped walk the dogs in the small yard behind the building. An old beagle named Pepper walked slowly beside him and leaned against his leg the whole time.
Page 5: By the third Saturday, Marcus knew every animal by name. He brought old towels from home for the dogs to sleep on. He made toys out of rolled-up newspaper for the cats.
Page 6: On his last Saturday, a family came in and adopted Pepper. Marcus felt happy and sad at the same time as he watched the beagle waddle out with his new family.
Page 7: The shelter worker took a photo of Marcus and put it on the volunteer board on the wall. You made a real difference, she told him. The animals noticed your kindness.
Page 8: Marcus did not know that helping others could make him feel so full inside. He signed up to volunteer every month. He brought his younger sister the next time.

Level D — The Great Egg Drop
============================================================
Page 1: Ms. Hall gave the class an exciting challenge — build a container that would protect a raw egg when dropped from the top of the school stairs. They had one week to prepare.
Page 2: Teams of three were allowed to use any materials they could bring from home. Finn, Dalia, and Rob spent two lunch breaks brainstorming before they settled on their design.
Page 3: They built a small box out of cardboard and filled it with bubble wrap on every side. Then they wrapped the egg in cotton balls and put it right in the center.
Page 4: Some teams used plastic bags full of water. One team used a parachute made from a trash bag. Another team used a tube packed tightly with foam noodles.
Page 5: On drop day, the whole class gathered at the top of the stairs. Ms. Hall dropped each container one at a time. The first two eggs cracked loudly on impact. The class groaned.
Page 6: When it was Finn's team's turn, Finn held his breath. Ms. Hall dropped the box. It bounced once, then stopped. Everyone went quiet as Rob opened the box.
Page 7: The egg was completely uncracked. The class erupted in cheers. Even teams whose eggs had cracked clapped for them.
Page 8: That afternoon, Finn wrote in his journal that failing at something and figuring out why is half of science. He thought the other half might be jumping up and down when it works.

Level D — The River Cleanup
============================================================
Page 1: When Lily walked along the path beside the river, she noticed how much trash was tangled in the reeds and floating near the banks. It made her feel very upset.
Page 2: She told her parents that night. Someone should clean that up, she said. Dad raised an eyebrow and said, Someone like who?
Page 3: Lily thought about it. She made some flyers on the computer and put them up around the neighborhood, asking people to come help on a Saturday morning.
Page 4: She was worried no one would show up. But that Saturday, twelve neighbors arrived with gloves and garbage bags. Even the elderly couple from down the street came.
Page 5: They spent three hours picking up cans, plastic bags, bottles, and other trash from the water and the muddy bank. The bags filled up quickly.
Page 6: When they were done, the river looked so much better. Light glinted off the clean water. A duck paddled by as if to say thank you.
Page 7: The local newspaper sent a reporter to take a photo. The headline the next day read: Ten-Year-Old Organizes Neighborhood River Cleanup. Lily's mom cut it out and framed it.
Page 8: But what mattered most to Lily was not the headline. It was knowing that the fish and ducks and herons would have a cleaner home because she had decided to do something.

Level D — The Night Hike
============================================================
Page 1: Dad had a surprise for Sasha and her brother Theo. They were going on a night hike in the forest behind the campsite, armed with headlamps and a thermos of hot chocolate.
Page 2: At first the dark felt big and thick, pressing in from all sides. Theo kept his flashlight pointed straight ahead and walked close to Dad.
Page 3: Dad told them to pause and turn off their headlamps. For a moment it was completely dark. Then their eyes slowly adjusted and the world reappeared in silver and shadow.
Page 4: Look up, Dad said softly. The trees opened above them, and the sky was packed with stars, more than either of them had ever seen.
Page 5: As they walked deeper into the forest, they heard owls calling from high in the branches. Frogs sang from a nearby pond. The forest was full of sound.
Page 6: Then Dad pointed his light at a tree trunk. A moth the size of a hand rested there, its wings patterned like two big eyes staring back at them.
Page 7: They stood very still and watched until the moth fluttered away into the dark. Sasha felt like they were in a different world, a secret one that only came alive at night.
Page 8: Back at the campsite, they wrapped their hands around warm mugs and talked about everything they had seen. Sasha decided that nighttime was not frightening. It was just the world wearing a different outfit.

Level D — The Sandwich Contest
============================================================
Page 1: On a rainy Saturday, Dad declared that there would be a family sandwich contest. Everyone would create the best sandwich they could, and Mom would judge it blindfolded.
Page 2: The rules were simple — only ingredients already in the kitchen, no help from anyone else, and the sandwich had to fit on one plate.
Page 3: Younger brother Eli made a peanut butter sandwich with banana slices and a drizzle of honey. It was simple but neat, and he arranged the banana slices in a smiley face.
Page 4: Dad made a towering club sandwich with turkey, cheese, tomato, lettuce, and a toothpick flag. It was so tall that it fell over before Mom could taste it. Dad called it abstract art.
Page 5: Nora took a long time planning hers. She made a grilled cheese with apple slices and a tiny bit of mustard. She had seen that combination in a cookbook once.
Page 6: Mom sat at the table and tasted all three sandwiches without peeking. She made thoughtful sounds with each bite, which made everyone nervous.
Page 7: After the third taste, she pulled off the blindfold. Nora's grilled cheese won for unexpected flavor. Eli's won for most cheerful presentation. Dad's won for most ambitious attempt.
Page 8: Everyone got a small prize — a sticker from the junk drawer. But they all agreed the best prize was eating three sandwiches for lunch and calling it a competition.

Page 2: Groups of four had thirty minutes to plan and set up their course. Then every team would run every course and vote on which one they liked best.

Page 8: Coach Wu said this was one of his favorite class days of the year. Ana decided that gym was a lot more fun when you got to make up your own rules.

Level D — The Flooded Basement
============================================================
Page 1: After three days of heavy rain, the Kim family discovered two inches of water in their basement. It was not a disaster, but it was a big mess that needed to be cleaned up.
Page 2: Mom and Dad put on rubber boots and went down to assess the damage. Dad set up a pump to remove the water. It made a loud sucking noise that the kids could hear from upstairs.
Page 3: Jake and his older sister Erin were told they could help by moving things up from the basement before anything else got wet.
Page 4: Jake carried boxes of old books while Erin moved the holiday decorations to the upstairs hallway. They made a pile in the living room that nearly reached the ceiling.
Page 5: Some things were damaged — a box of old photos had gotten soggy at the bottom, and a stack of board games had warped lids. Jake felt sad about the photos.
Page 6: But Mom held one of the damp photos up to the light. Most of them are fine, she said. A little water cannot erase a memory. They spread the photos out to dry on towels.
Page 7: By evening, the basement was empty and drying out. The family sat on the living room floor surrounded by piles of saved belongings and ate pizza because nobody had cooked dinner.
Page 8: Jake thought about the photos drying on the towels. He decided that when everything was sorted, he would ask Mom to make a real photo album so the pictures would be safe forever.

Level E — The Robot Project
============================================================
Page 1: When the teacher announced that the class would spend a month building a simple robot from a kit, Theo was the most excited student in the room. He had been reading about robotics for two years and had been waiting for exactly this kind of challenge.
Page 2: His partner was a quiet girl named Ingrid who had never built anything mechanical before. On the first day, she opened the kit box, looked at the hundreds of tiny pieces, and nearly put the lid back on.
Page 3: Theo dove straight into the instructions, reading ahead and skipping steps. Parts kept going in the wrong place and having to be pulled apart. Ingrid watched and noticed a pattern. You are rushing, she said gently.
Page 4: Theo stopped and looked at the mess of parts in front of him. He took a breath. Ingrid picked up the instruction sheet and read step one aloud. Just step one. They did it together slowly.
Page 5: It turned out that Ingrid was very good at keeping track of small parts, organizing the workspace, and catching mistakes before they became big problems. Theo was good at understanding how the pieces fit together mechanically.
Page 6: Over the next three weeks, their robot took shape. It had two wheels, a sensor that stopped it from bumping into walls, and a small arm that could push a block across the floor.
Page 7: On demonstration day, nearly every robot in the class bumped into something or stopped short. Theo and Ingrid's robot smoothly navigated the cardboard maze on the first try. The class applauded.
Page 8: Afterward, Ingrid said she wanted to learn more about coding so the robot could do more complicated things. Theo handed her the book he had been reading. That, he thought, was how the best partnerships worked — each person teaching the other something new.

Level E — The Community Garden
============================================================
Page 1: The empty lot on Maple Street had been covered in weeds and broken glass for as long as anyone in the neighborhood could remember. Then one spring, a woman named Ms. Okafor put up a sign that said Community Garden Coming Soon! and everything changed.
Page 2: Twelve families signed up to help. They spent two weekends clearing the weeds, hauling away the trash, and testing the soil. It was backbreaking work, but nobody complained because they could already imagine what it would look like.
Page 3: Each family was given a small plot to grow whatever they wanted. The Chens planted bok choy and snow peas. The Garcias planted tomatoes, peppers, and herbs. An older man named Mr. Singh planted five different varieties of squash.
Page 4: Ten-year-old Dani and her mom planted sunflowers along the fence because, as Dani said, every garden needs something tall and happy-looking to greet you when you walk in.
Page 5: By July, the lot had been transformed. It was green and buzzing with bees, smelling of basil and warm earth. Neighbors who had never spoken much to each other found themselves chatting over the fence between their plots.
Page 6: There were setbacks too — a heat wave scorched the lettuce in August, and a family of rabbits found a gap under the fence and ate half of Mr. Singh's squash. But people shared their harvests and helped each other replant.
Page 7: In the fall, the garden held a harvest festival. People brought dishes made entirely from things they had grown. Dani made sunflower seed brittle and gave a piece to every person who came through the gate.
Page 8: Standing at the edge of the lot that evening, watching neighbors talk and laugh and share food under strings of warm lights, Dani thought that the most important thing they had grown all summer was not a vegetable at all.

Level E — The Broken Bridge
============================================================
Page 1: The old footbridge over the creek at the edge of town had been closed for two years with a chain and a sign that said Unsafe — Do Not Cross. For most people it was easy to ignore. For Maya and her friends it was the fastest way to the nature trail on the other side.
Page 2: One afternoon Maya stopped and really looked at the bridge for the first time. Some of the boards were cracked, a few were missing, and the rope railing had frayed in places. But the main beams looked solid.
Page 3: She brought it up at dinner that night. Dad, who was an engineer, talked with her about the difference between cosmetic damage and structural damage. He explained that something can look bad but still be fundamentally sound, or it can look fine and be secretly dangerous.
Page 4: That weekend, Dad walked to the bridge with Maya and a clipboard. He spent an hour looking carefully at every part, pressing on wood, testing the railing posts, examining the bolts at each end. He made notes.
Page 5: The structure is fine, he said finally. It just needs new boards and new rope. We could fix this ourselves if we got permission and a few volunteers. Maya's eyes lit up like two small suns.
Page 6: She wrote a letter to the town council with Dad's inspection notes attached. Three weeks later, she stood in the council chambers and read the letter out loud. The council voted unanimously to approve the repair project.
Page 7: On a cool October Saturday, twelve volunteers — including Maya's friends, four parents, and two retired neighbors — replaced every damaged board and all the railing rope. By noon, the chain was down and the sign was gone.
Page 8: Maya was the first one across. The boards were solid under her feet, the railing firm in her hand. On the other side she turned around and looked back at the group cheering from the near bank. She thought: a thing that seems impossible is often just a problem waiting for someone to look at it closely enough.

Level E — The Long Winter
============================================================
Page 1: The winter that Petra turned ten was the longest and coldest anyone in the town could remember. By February, the snow was up to the windowsills and school had been cancelled for eight days in a row. The whole family was restless.
Page 2: Petra's little brother Soren had run out of things to do. He had built every LEGO set twice, finished all his library books, and watched every cartoon he was allowed to watch. He trailed after Petra asking for ideas.
Page 3: Petra decided to take the situation seriously. She got out a large piece of paper and drew a grid. At the top she wrote The Long Winter Activity Plan. She and Soren filled every box together.
Page 4: They learned origami from a library book and folded a hundred paper cranes, which they hung from the bedroom ceiling. They taught themselves to play chess using a dusty set from the back of the closet and watched videos to learn the rules.
Page 5: They built a fort in the living room so elaborate that it had rooms, a skylight made from a clear shower curtain, and a snack station. Mom and Dad were formally invited in for a candlelight tea party.
Page 6: Petra taught Soren to knit, which she had learned from their grandmother the previous summer. His first scarf was lumpy and uneven, and he was incredibly proud of it. He gave it to the dog.
Page 7: On the last snow day, they made a book together. Petra wrote and Soren drew pictures. It was called Everything We Learned During the Long Winter and had thirty-two pages. Grandma called it a masterpiece.
Page 8: When the snow finally melted and they ran outside blinking into the cold sunshine, Petra realized something surprising. She was actually a little sad the long winter was over. She had never spent so much time making things, and she was not sure she wanted to stop.

Level E — The Old Photograph
============================================================
Page 1: While helping her grandmother sort through boxes in the spare room, twelve-year-old Lia found a black-and-white photograph tucked inside an old envelope. It showed a young woman standing in front of a storefront, arms crossed, smiling directly at the camera with great confidence.
Page 2: Who is this? Lia asked. Grandma took the photo and was quiet for a moment. That is my mother, she said finally. Your great-grandmother. She owned that bakery for thirty years.
Page 3: Lia looked more closely. The sign above the door read Hana's Bread. The woman in the photo was young — maybe thirty — with her hair pinned back and flour on her apron. She looked like someone who knew exactly what she was doing.
Page 4: She came to this country with nothing, Grandma said, sitting down on the edge of the bed. She spoke no English. She had no money. But she knew how to bake. So she started small — selling bread from a basket at the market.
Page 5: Grandma told the story slowly, pausing sometimes to remember details. How Hana had saved every coin for two years before renting a tiny space. How she had learned English from her customers. How people lined up around the block on Sunday mornings for her poppy seed rolls.
Page 6: Did you work there? Lia asked. Every day after school, said Grandma. I folded boxes and swept floors and eventually learned to braid the challah. She taught me that good work done with care is never wasted. Not even the sweeping.
Page 7: Lia turned the photo over. On the back, in faded pencil, someone had written: Hana, 1954. First day open. She thought about everything she did not know about her own family — all the stories folded into envelopes in boxes in spare rooms.
Page 8: Can I keep this? Lia asked. Grandma shook her head gently. It belongs with the family, she said. But I will tell you what — let's put it in a frame. Things worth remembering should not be hidden in boxes. Lia nodded and thought she was beginning to understand what that meant.

Level E — The Audition
============================================================
Page 1: Jonah had never sung in front of anyone other than his mother, his bedroom mirror, and occasionally his confused golden retriever. When his music teacher announced that the city youth choir was holding open auditions, he spent three days pretending he had not heard the news.
Page 2: It was his friend Bree who finally called him out. You want to go, she said, not as a question but as a fact. She had heard him singing in the hallway once, clearly not realizing anyone was near. You are really good, Jonah. You should do it.
Page 3: He told himself he was only going to watch. He sat in the back of the community center and watched eleven other kids walk up to the piano, sing their sixteen bars, and receive a nod from the director. Some were technically perfect. A few were shaky but earnest.
Page 4: When the volunteer called his name — he had not even remembered signing up on the clipboard by the door — Jonah's legs stood up before his brain gave permission. He walked to the front of the room as if watching himself from a distance.
Page 5: The accompanist asked what he was singing. Jonah named the song he had practiced in his bedroom for two years, a folk melody his grandmother had taught him. The accompanist found it in her book and began to play.
Page 6: For the first four notes, Jonah's voice felt thin and faraway. Then something shifted. He stopped watching himself from a distance and just — sang. He thought about his grandmother's kitchen, and Sunday mornings, and the way the song felt in his chest, not in his throat.
Page 7: When he finished, the room was very quiet. The director made a note on her clipboard. Thank you, Jonah, she said. That was lovely. The word lovely landed somewhere in his ribcage and stayed there.
Page 8: Two weeks later, a letter arrived in the mail. He had made the choir. Bree heard him shout from two houses away and came over immediately. His golden retriever, who had always been his most loyal audience, wagged his tail as if he had known all along.

Level E — The Hurricane Season
============================================================
Page 1: Mara had lived in Florida her whole life, which meant she had spent every August and September watching weather maps and listening to adults talk about storm tracks and storm surges and emergency preparedness. Most years nothing came close. The summer she was eleven, one did.
Page 2: The storm was named Ida and it was a Category 2 when it made landfall. Their neighborhood was not in the evacuation zone, but the family still prepared carefully — filling bathtubs with water, charging every device, buying extra canned food, and boarding up the biggest windows.
Page 3: Dad explained each step as they did it, not to frighten her but because, he said, understanding what you are doing and why makes you feel less powerless. Mara wrote down every step in a small notebook she kept in her pocket.
Page 4: The storm arrived at night. Mara lay in her bed listening to the wind build from a hum to a howl to something that rattled the walls and made the whole house feel like a boat on an enormous dark ocean.
Page 5: She was not exactly scared. She was something more complicated than scared — intensely aware of how small the house was and how large the world outside it was, and how extraordinary it was that four walls and a roof could hold against something so vast.
Page 6: The power went out at midnight. They sat together in the living room by flashlight, playing cards and telling stories. The wind reached its peak at around three in the morning. Then slowly, over an hour, it began to ease.
Page 7: In the morning they walked outside to assess the yard. A large oak branch had crushed the garden shed. Shingles were scattered across the lawn. A neighbor's trash can sat in the middle of their driveway. But the house was intact, and everyone was safe.
Page 8: Mara looked at her notebook full of preparation steps. She thought about adding a new page — one about what it felt like to sit inside a storm and know you had done everything you could. Some lessons, she decided, were too important to leave unwritten.

Level E — The Best Worst Day
============================================================
Page 1: The day of the regional cross-country meet, everything went wrong. Theo woke up with a sore throat. The family car wouldn't start. His dad's phone died with the race address still on it, and they spent twenty frantic minutes finding a neighbor who knew the park.
Page 2: They arrived eleven minutes before his heat. Theo had no time to warm up properly, no time to find his teammates, and barely enough time to pin his race number to his shirt before the starter called all runners to the line.
Page 3: He had trained for this race for eight weeks. He had run the course distances in practice. He knew his pacing strategy and had it memorized like a song. But standing at the starting line with a raw throat and cold legs, every bit of preparation felt far away.
Page 4: When the gun went off, Theo started too fast — he could feel it immediately but could not stop himself. The first half mile felt good in the wrong way, the kind of good that promises to become pain. By mile one he was breathing too hard.
Page 5: By mile two, three runners had passed him. His legs were heavy. His throat burned. He thought about stopping, walking, pretending to be injured, all the forms of giving up that the mind offers when the body is struggling.
Page 6: Then he passed a younger kid who had stopped to tie a shoe. The kid looked up at Theo struggling past and said, You've got it! with complete sincerity. Something about that voice — a stranger cheering for him with zero reason to — pulled Theo back into his body.
Page 7: He did not win. He finished ninth out of forty-two, which was three places behind his practice goal. His coach shook his hand and said, That was a real race. You learned more today than in any training run this season.
Page 8: On the ride home, Theo sat in the back seat with his head against the window. His dad asked how he felt. Tired, Theo said. And sort of proud. Of what? his dad asked. Of finishing, Theo said. When finishing was the hardest possible option.

Level E — The Migration
============================================================
Page 1: When Dr. Reyes announced that the class would track monarch butterfly migration for their fall science project, most students thought it would be easy. You look up butterflies and write it down, someone said. It turned out to be much more complicated and much more interesting than that.
Page 2: Lily and her partner James were assigned to research the biology of migration itself — not just where the butterflies went, but how they knew to go there. The question seemed simple until they started reading and discovered that nobody fully understood the answer.
Page 3: Monarch butterflies fly up to three thousand miles from Canada to a small mountain forest in Mexico. They have never made the trip before — the generation that migrates is four generations removed from the last one that made the journey. Yet they find the exact same trees.
Page 4: Scientists believe they use the angle of sunlight as a compass, corrected by an internal clock. They may also sense the Earth's magnetic field. Some researchers think they even navigate by the polarization of light that human eyes cannot perceive.
Page 5: Lily stayed up one night reading studies on her tablet, genuinely unable to stop. James filled three pages of notes. At school the next day they could not talk about anything else, which annoyed their other friends but delighted Dr. Reyes.
Page 6: For their presentation, they built a giant paper mural showing the migration route, with arrows marking the waypoints. They hung orange paper butterflies from the ceiling above the mural using fishing line so they appeared to be flying.
Page 7: During the presentation, James explained the navigation science while Lily showed the route. Then Lily asked the class a question she had not been able to stop thinking about: If the butterfly has never made this journey before, and its parents have never made it, what is carrying the memory? The class was quiet for a long time.
Page 8: Dr. Reyes told them that was one of the great questions of biology — a question still being studied at universities around the world. Lily wrote that sentence down in her notebook and underlined it. She thought that knowing the right questions might matter even more than knowing all the answers.

Level E — The Apology
============================================================
Page 1: At lunch on a Tuesday, in front of twelve people, Nina said something unkind about her best friend Cora's project — the one Cora had spent three weeks building. It was a model of a tidal power generator made entirely from cardboard and rubber bands, and Nina said it looked like something a kindergartner would make. Everyone at the table laughed.
Page 2: Cora's face changed in a way Nina had never seen before. Not angry, exactly. More like something had been quietly broken. She picked up her lunch tray and moved to another table without saying anything.
Page 3: Nina told herself it was just a joke. Cora was too sensitive. It wasn't a big deal. She thought this for approximately three hours before she admitted to herself, lying on her bed staring at the ceiling, that it had been unkind and she had known it was unkind when she said it.
Page 4: The hard part was not knowing it was wrong. The hard part was that apologizing meant admitting, out loud, that she had chosen to be mean. That she had wanted the table to laugh more than she had wanted to protect her friend. That was a difficult truth to hold.
Page 5: She called Cora that evening. Cora answered but didn't say anything, just waited. Nina had practiced what to say and all of it sounded hollow now. She started over. I said something cruel today because I wanted people to laugh. It wasn't true and it wasn't kind and I am genuinely sorry.
Page 6: There was a long pause. Cora said, You really hurt my feelings. Nina said, I know. I'm sorry. Not as an explanation or a defense, just the two words, plain.
Page 7: Cora did not immediately say it was okay, because it was not immediately okay. But she said, Thank you for calling. That meant a lot. They talked for another twenty minutes about other things, carefully, the way people do when they are beginning to mend something.
Page 8: At school the next day, Nina sat with Cora at lunch. Nobody mentioned the day before. But Nina looked at the tidal generator model Cora had brought in for class and said, Actually, I think it's impressive. And she meant it. Cora smiled. Some apologies, Nina learned, are only the first step of a longer road.
